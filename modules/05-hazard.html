⚠ CRITICAL MISSION RISK
Global dust storms (GDS) occur on Mars roughly every 2–3 Mars years (3.8–5.7 Earth years). During a GDS, atmospheric opacity (tau) reaches 5–8, reducing solar irradiance at the surface by up to 99%. Solar-powered systems fail within days. The 2018 GDS killed the Opportunity rover after 15 years of operation. Any Mars surface mission must plan for 3–6 month power blackouts.

⚡ POWER STRATEGY REQUIREMENT
Solar power alone is insufficient for either site. Recommended: RTG (Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generator) as primary power for drill rig and critical systems, with solar panels as supplementary. RTGs produce ~100W continuously regardless of dust opacity. Multi-Mission RTG (MMRTG) provides 110W beginning-of-mission — 2 units minimum per site. Estimated cost: $80M per MMRTG.
RTG primary power
2× MMRTG per site, 110W each. Provides baseline power through any dust event indefinitely.
CRITICAL
Dust wiper systems
Active electrostatic dust removal on all solar panels. Passive wipers on optical sensors. Reduces non-storm tau impact by ~40%.
RECOMMENDED
Storm hibernation protocol
Automated shutdown of non-critical systems when tau exceeds 2.0. Drill operations suspended. RTG maintains data relay and thermal systems only.
REQUIRED
Sealed instrument enclosures
All precision instruments in dust-sealed enclosures. Dust particle size on Mars 1–3 μm — fine enough to penetrate standard IP65 seals.
CRITICAL
Elevated panel mounts
Solar panels on 3m masts above surface dust accumulation layer. Nili Fossae graben floor excluded — panels on rim only.
DESIGN FEATURE
Dust storm early warning
MCS-equivalent orbital monitor for real-time tau measurement. 48-hour advance warning triggers hibernation protocol automatically.

⚠ CHRONIC MISSION RISK
Mars has no global magnetic field and a thin atmosphere (~0.6% of Earth's). The surface receives ~0.64 mSv/day from galactic cosmic rays (GCR) — about 25× Earth sea-level dose. Solar particle events (SPEs) can spike dose rates to lethal levels within hours. For robotic missions this primarily affects electronics reliability — total ionising dose (TID) limits component lifetime.

GCR dose falls rapidly with regolith depth. Surface electronics experience 640 mSv/day; burying electronics at 2m reduces dose to ~120 mSv/day. Drill downhole tools at 5+ m depth effectively shielded. SPE events spike surface dose — short duration (hours to days) but can exceed 2,000 mSv/hr.
Radiation-hardened electronics
All processors, memory, and sensors specified to ≥1 Mrad TID. Standard commercial components rated ~10 krad — would fail within weeks.
CRITICAL
Buried electronics vault
Primary computing buried 1–2m below surface inside a steel vault. Regolith provides ~15× dose reduction vs surface. Nili graben walls provide additional partial shielding.
CRITICAL
SPE storm shelter
MAVEN-derived SPE early warning (15–60 min lead time). Non-critical systems powered down. Electronics vault provides passive shielding during events.
REQUIRED
Redundant flight computers
Triple-redundant voting architecture. If one processor accumulates bit errors from radiation damage, the other two override. Mars rover heritage design.
REQUIRED
Annual electronics refresh
Mission design includes spare electronics modules launched with Phase 2 hardware for on-site replacement of radiation-damaged components after year 5.
DESIGN FEATURE
Nili graben advantage
The graben rim walls at Nili Fossae provide 15–20° of geometric shielding for low-elevation SPE particles. Estimated 8–12% dose reduction vs open plains.
NILI ADVANTAGE
◆ RADIATION CONCLUSION
Radiation hazard is nearly identical at both sites — the ~3% difference in atmospheric column between Tharsis (higher elevation) and Nili Fossae is negligible. Both require full rad-hard electronics specification. The primary radiation mitigation is the same for both: bury computing hardware, use rad-hard components, and maintain real-time SPE monitoring via orbital assets. Neither site has a significant radiation advantage — design spec dominates over geography.
