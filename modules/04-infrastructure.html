KEY INSIGHT
The $1,080M drilling cost difference is almost entirely driven by depth. Tharsis's 8 km rig is nearly twice the mass of Nili Fossae's 5 km rig — and mass-to-Mars is the dominant cost driver. Nili Fossae's shallow serpentinite target is the most cost-efficient first drill on Mars.

NUCLEAR FISSION POWER SYSTEMS

Space Nuclear Reactors
for Mars Surface Operations

Solar panels fail during global dust storms and generate ~40% of Earth output even in clear Martian conditions. Nuclear fission is the only reliable primary power source for a robotic wildcat drilling mission. Two systems are mission-relevant: the heritage SNAP-10A (currently in polar orbit since 1965) and NASA's modern Kilopower/KRUSTY programme — both suited to Stirling engine coupling for maximum thermal efficiency.

ORBITAL ASSET · FLIGHT-PROVEN HARDWARE
The SNAP-10A is not just a concept — a flight unit was launched April 3, 1965 aboard Atlas Agena D and remains in a 700 nautical mile retrograde polar orbit expected to persist for approximately 1,000 years. It operated for 43 days producing 500W electrical before a remote electrical control failure — not a reactor failure. The reactor itself is intact. The 30 kW(t) thermal output coupled with a modern Stirling engine at ~30% efficiency would yield ~9 kW(e) — sufficient power for a Phase 1 Mars wildcat survey and shallow drill operation.

The original SNAP-10A used thermoelectric conversion at 1.7% efficiency. Replacing with a modern free-piston Stirling convertor at ~30% efficiency would extract ~9 kW(e) from the same 30 kW(t) reactor — a 18× improvement using 60-year-old flight-proven hardware. The reactor design needs no development. Only the power conversion side requires engineering. Buried 2m below Mars surface reduces radiation dose to surface electronics by ~15×.

KRUSTY demonstrated full-power operations in 2018 at Nevada National Security Site — the first US fission reactor test since 1965. Passive sodium heat pipes (no moving parts in primary loop) coupled to Stirling convertors. Four 10 kW(e) units provide 40 kW continuous — sufficient for full drill rig, ISRU processing, and communications at either Mars site. NASA's Moon to Mars power architecture baseline as of 2023.

POWER ARCHITECTURE RECOMMENDATION
4× Kilopower units (40 kW continuous) as primary, 2× MMRTG (220W) as emergency backup. Power budget: drill rig motor ~15 kW, MWD logging ~2 kW, ISRU processing ~10 kW, communications ~3 kW, thermal control ~8 kW, margin ~2 kW. The SNAP-10A + Stirling path represents the fastest route to flight hardware — reactor design is already space-proven, only power conversion requires development. Nuclear power system cost estimate: $320M per site, included in Phase 2 costs above.
